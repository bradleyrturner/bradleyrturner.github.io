## Preparing for Submission

**When Short Stories are Long: Narrative Strategy Under Evaluative Uncertainty** *(working title)*    
*With Georg Rilinger (equal authorship)*  

Scholars tend to view narratives as heuristics that reduce complexity. Clear, consistent, and well-supported stories are seen as most effective in shaping market valuation. Yet activist short sellers, financial-market contrarians who depend on rapid market revaluations, often publish sprawling, rhetorically complex reports with uneven evidence. Why? Leveraging qualitative coding and machine learning to analyze over 700 reports, financial data, media coverage and interviews with activsist short sellers, we explain this pattern as the function of a dual mandate: To persuade and coordinate heterogeneous audiences. To move the market, short sellers must both simplify complexity and coordinate multiple, interdependent investor audiences. This dual challenge becomes more severe under evaluative uncertainty, when standards for evaluating firm value are fragmented, contested, or absent. When evaluative uncertainty is high, short sellers encode information for multiple audiences, helping each make second-order inferences about how others will respond. To do so, they strategically structure complex reports with multiple storylines that either appeal to different evaluative frames or invoke a higher-order moral standard. When uncertainty is lower, they are more inclined to exploit the conventional clarity and fit with established standards. By linking narrative form to audience coordination under uncertainty, this paper contributes to theories of classification, narrative persuasion, and valuation in financial markets.

**Contesting Consensus: Activist Short Sellers' Identity as a Resource** *(working title)*  

This paper explores how professionals cultivate identities to contest consensus. I leverage archival data, including coverage by the mainstream and financial press, to construct a 35-year case study of activist short selling (1990–2025) and  interviews with short sellers. I explore how successful intervention in a field may depend combined deployment and defiance of mainstream competencies and norms.

**Stories Within Stories: How Entrepreneurs' Concrete Anecdotes Enhance Versus Undermine Credibility** *(working title)*    

This paper contributes to a debate about the efficacy of strategic storytelling for organizations and entrepreneurs. Leveraging qualitative analysis of 100 entrepreneur-investor interactions at three pitch events, I focuses on the concrete anecdotes in pitches, the symbolic and persuasive work they accomplish, and how investors respond to them. I find that not all entrepreneurs tell stories, not all stories resonate with investors, and even resonant stories can be dismissed as irrelevant for the investment decision. Based on the lines of questions that entrepreneurs receive from investors, I hypothesize that the familiar Aha! moments and origin stories are less compelling than more distinctive, detailed stories about personal commitment, customer problems, and product trials. Building on existing research, I theorize about when stories strengthen versus undermine credibility, arguing that it depends on whether investors perceive them as complements or substitutes for data and argument claims. By unpacking the high-level concept of storytelling, and analyzing the full range of investor responses to it, I contribute to research on entrepreneurship, impression management, and narrative.
